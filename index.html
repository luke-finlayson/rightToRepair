The Right to Repair

So, Whats the deal?

A few decades ago, if something you owned broke, you could fix it or get your neighbour to do it for you. If neither of those were options, then you could find some local repair shop, and they would fix it for you at a low price.

These days, those options are quickly disappearing, and it's becoming harder to repair electronic equipment, such as computers, phones and cars.

Unfortunately, this is the result of planned obsolescence. Manufacturers are deliberately designing their products to only last a few years at most. They refuse to publish any repair documentation, suggesting that if your product breaks, you take it to one of their authorised 'repair' shops. The 'Right to Repair' movement was created to stop this from continuing. The people involved in the campaign have been attempting to pass new legislation laws for several years now; however large companies, such as Apple, have been successfully blocking them.

Ok, but why do we have to worry?

Globally, 50 million tons of electronic waste is generated every year, that's the weight of over 8000 elephants! Repairing electronic products gets them back into the hands of people who can keep using them. It keeps the rubbish out of the landfills and reduces the increasing problem of pollution.

Manufacturers, by deliberately designing their products to be unusable after just a few years, are forcing increasing amounts of e-waste into the system

This is an increasing problem in developing countries, and they are unable to source sufficient funds or resources to dispose of e-waste properly. Many developed countries find it cheaper and easier to ship their e-waste to those developing countries, adding to the amount of e-waste undergoing unsafe disposal. When e-waste undergoes hazardous disposal, it produces dangerous chemicals such as lead, arsenic, chromium, and dioxin which leach into the environment causing environmental degradation and harms people and animals in the vicinity.

How did this all get started?

The concept of the right to repair mainly come from the United States, in 2012 Massachusetts passed the 'Motor Vehicles Owners Right to Repair Act' which forced manufacturers to provide adequate documentation and information to allow anyone to repair their vehicles. Motivated by these events The Right to Repair Association was founded in 2013 to bring the same approach to electronics.

Manufacturers had been enforcing the practice where the only way to repair a device would be to go to one of their authorised services or OEM (Original Equipment Manufacturer). This prevents any third-party from servicing their devices. The Right to Repair Association began to fight for state bills in the US to be passed, to allow consumers to repair their devices.

What can I do to Help?

Currently, despite extensive support, manufacturers are still opposing the right to repair movement. However, you don't have to do as they suggest. Instead of taking your broken device to the Manufacturer, try instead of taking it to an independent repair shop. Or repair the device yourself by following the easy, clear and free repair guides put together by the team at ifixit.com.

If you live in the US, you can go to repair.org to contact your state legistators to make the Right to Repair happen
